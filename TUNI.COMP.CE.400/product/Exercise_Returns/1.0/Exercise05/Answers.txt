7. For CPUs, MIPS was measured in this exercise. Why this gives us a value differing from calculated MIPS?
 The calculated MIPS was done based on the information given from the number of cores and their clock frequencies, and number of cycles per multiplication without taking into account the physical memory speed. On the other hand, the measured MIPS reflects real execution, which includes pipeline stalls, cache effects, OS overhead, and dynamic frequency changes. Because of these factors, the measured MIPS differs from the calculated MIPS.
 
8. What is probably the most important thing that the used MIPS measurements did not take into account?
HINT: We told you the answer in the instructions, but we are just making sure you understand.
The MIPS measurements did not include memory access effects, they ignored the memory bottleneck and only measured register-based multiplication performance.

10. Why did we assume that roughly 60% of the execution was on the FPGA rather than the ARM CPU?
HINT: Look at the profiling results of exercise 1, or instructions of exercise 3.
Both the profiling results from Exercise 1 and the state-machine diagram in Exercise 3 show that intra prediction—especially angular prediction—takes more than 60% of the total encoding time. Since intra prediction is the part accelerated on the FPGA, we model the system assuming that roughly 60% of the execution is transferred from the ARM CPU to the FPGA.
